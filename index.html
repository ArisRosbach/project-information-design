Mozambique's tax treaties

Mozambique, a country that probably does not immediately ring a bell, but in which a lot of the world is invested in. They are located on top of a gas bubble, which generates a lot of interest.

But despite the great interest in Mozambique, they miss out on a lot of tax. Tax is more important than you think. Especially for a country that can put money to good use for their development.

Tax evasion vs Tax avoidance?

Before we start with the complicated story, it might be useful to know what the difference is between tax evasion and tax avoidance.

Tax evasion is the failure to (fully) comply with tax obligations. For example, by withholding information or providing incorrect information when filing a tax return. Tax evasion is illegal.

Tax avoidance is done, for example, by setting up so-called letterbox companies. The weird thing is, this is legal. Later we will discuss these letterbox companies in more detail.

Tax treaties

Tax treaties can play a big part when it comes to less tax revenue. But what are tax treaties?

Countries make mutual agreements in so-called tax treaties. These agreements are about taxing individuals and companies that invest and operate cross-border. The purpose is to agree on which government is going to tax where and when, and to exchange information between the two governments.

A frequently cited reason for concluding them is an expected increase in investments between the two countries. Tax treaties encourage investment by reducing 'barriers' to economic traffic between the countries, such as levying withholding tax. However, lowering the withholding tax also results in less direct tax revenue for the signatory countries.

Tax havens & letterbox companies

Countries with a favorable tax climate are called tax havens. These are countries with a very favorable tax system, so you pay less tax on capital, income, and profit. Tax havens play a major role when it comes to tax avoidance.

Tax avoidance is mainly done by letterbox companies. These are companies that are set up in tax havens to take advantage of this, but in fact, there is only a postal address or an empty office. The company is based in a country on paper, but no economic activities take place in that country.

Why is there so much fuss about tax havens?

Part of the profits of companies is funneled away to a letterbox company in a tax haven. As a result, the country where this profit was made misses out on tax revenue, because this tax is paid in the tax haven.

Why Mozambique?

Mozambique possesses a very important raw material. Namely, natural gas which was found in 2010. Gas and oil multinationals ENI from Italy and Anadarko from the U.S.A. made the discovery just off the coast of Cabo Delgado, Mozambique's northernmost province. The discovery was worth hundreds of billions of dollars.

You would think that Mozambique would be very wealthy because of the discovery of gas. The reality of today's Mozambique is that the country is suffering from a phenomenon known as the presource curse. This occurs when countries have large natural resources, yet display little to no economic growth, as well as increasing inequality, (armed) conflicts, and corruption.

According to the Norwegian embassy's research team, Mozambique's current crisis is so closely related to the discovery of gas that it refers to it as a presource curse. One which manifests itself before a single drop of liquefied gas has been exported.

Mozambique's current government has been forced to implement budget cuts. It cannot pay its rapidly increasing debt and does not have access to the International Monetary Funds because the loans the country had kept secret.

Countries that are involved with Mozambique

What stands out is that South Africa, Mauritius, the United Arab Emirates, Portugal, and India turn out to be the biggest sources of foreign direct investors and have a tax treaty with Mozambique.

The 3 anti-abuse measures

But how can tax avoidance be prevented?

This can be done with the help of the 3 'anti-abuse measures'.

1

A principal purpose test (PPT)

This test determines whether the main purpose of the potential partner's economic activity is to obtain treaty benefits. When found to be, treaty benefits may be denied.

2

A full limitation on benefits (LOB) clause

Criteria are established that must be met to qualify for treaty benefits.

3

A combination of 1 and 2 (SLOB)

A principal purpose test is done and criteria must be met to qualify for treaty benefits.

The first measure, PPT, is the most popular to be applied. The danger, however, is that such a test is very subjective. This can cause inconsistencies and uncertainties. SLOB is therefore the best one to apply for low-income countries.

Of the current treaties in Mozambique, 2 contain the PPT measure, those with India and Ethiopia. This is a positive step, but in this way, Mozambique is not protected against the greatest abuse. The UAE and Mauritius have the two most problematic treaties and there are no further measures against them.

Advice against tax avoidance

To help Mozambique fight tax avoidance, they can take measures in the current and new tax treaties. Scroll to the right to find a few pieces of advice for Mozambique.

1

Pause any current or future tax treaty negotiations

All tax treaty negotiations that Mozambique is engaged in must be postponed. The potential tax losses entailed by a tax treaty should be reconsidered. It is also not recommended to conclude a tax treaty with known conduit countries or tax havens.

2

Design a clear tax treaties policy

To properly negotiate tax treaties, Mozambique can design a tax treaty model that represents the ideal scenario for Mozambique.

3

Conduct a full review of the current tax treaties network

The current tax treaty network needs to be reassessed, looking at all aspects of the treaty. This is to assess whether the treaty should be renegotiated or terminated.

4

Renegotiate unfavorable tax treaties

Almost all of Mozambique's current treaties significantly reduce withholding tax rates for interest, dividends, and royalties. Mozambique's government may renegotiate its worst tax treaties to include higher withholding tax rates and improve other provisions that could limit Mozambique's taxable deductions. If renegotiation fails, Mozambique could consider ending its most problematic tax treaties, particularly those with the UAE and Mauritius. Termination has proven to be a successful route to renegotiation for some countries and can be used as a tactic to gain favorable benefits, but only if necessary.
